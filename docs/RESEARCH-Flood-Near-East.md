## 1. Genesis 6-9 (Noah)

### Dating of Textual Traditions

The flood narrative in Genesis is widely recognized as a composite text woven from at least two literary sources identified by the Documentary Hypothesis:

- **Priestly source (P):** Generally dated to the exilic or early post-exilic period, ca. 6th-5th century BCE. P provides the precise chronological framework, the detailed ark dimensions, and the covenant theology.
- **Yahwist source (J):** Generally dated to the monarchic period, ca. 10th-9th century BCE (though some recent scholars such as Joel Baden and Jan Christian Gertz push it later, to the 7th century or even exilic period). J provides the anthropomorphic depiction of YHWH, the distinction between clean and unclean animals (7 pairs vs. 1 pair), and the sacrificial aftermath.
- **Elohist source (E):** Most scholars find little or no independent E material in the flood narrative specifically. The flood account is primarily a J + P composite.

The final redaction of the Pentateuch is generally placed in the 5th-4th century BCE.

### Key Hebrew Terms

- **mabbul:** A term used almost exclusively for the Genesis flood (only other occurrence: Psalm 29:10). Its etymology is debated; it may derive from a root meaning "to destroy" or be cognate with Akkadian *abūbu* ("deluge," a cosmic, divinely sent flood). Its restricted usage suggests it denotes a uniquely cosmic, unrepeatable cataclysm.
- **tebah:** The word for Noah's vessel, used elsewhere in the Hebrew Bible only for the basket of Moses (Exodus 2:3-5). It is *not* the normal Hebrew word for "ship" (*oniyyah*). The term may be an Egyptian loanword (*db.t*, "box, chest"), emphasizing that the ark is not a navigable ship but a sealed container — a floating house of divine preservation.

### Key Narrative Details

| Element | J Source | P Source |
|---|---|---|
| **Reason for flood** | Wickedness of humanity (6:5-7) | Earth "corrupted" and filled with violence (6:11-13) |
| **Animals** | 7 pairs clean, 1 pair unclean (7:2-3) | 1 pair of every kind (6:19-20) |
| **Ark dimensions** | Not specified | 300 x 50 x 30 cubits (~450 x 75 x 45 feet); 3 decks (6:15-16) |
| **Flood mechanism** | Rain, 40 days and 40 nights (7:4, 12) | Fountains of the great deep + windows of heaven; cosmic waters (7:11) |
| **Flood duration** | 40 days rain + drying period | 150 days rising, 150 days receding; total ~1 year and 10 days |
| **Bird sequence** | Raven, then dove sent three times (8:6-12) | (Incorporated into P framework) |
| **Landing site** | Not named specifically | Mountains of Ararat — a region, not a single peak (8:4) |
| **Aftermath** | Noah builds altar, offers burnt offerings; YHWH smells "pleasing aroma" (8:20-21) | God establishes covenant (*berit*) with Noah; rainbow as sign (9:8-17) |
| **Covenant content** | YHWH's internal resolve never again to curse the ground (8:21) | Formal covenant: never again a flood; permission to eat meat; prohibition on consuming blood; capital punishment for murder (9:1-17) |

### The Ark's Dimensions and Significance

P's ark (300 x 50 x 30 cubits) has a 6:1 length-to-width ratio, which is remarkably seaworthy for a barge-like vessel. The proportions contrast sharply with Utnapishtim's cubic vessel in Gilgamesh XI. The three-deck internal structure and the single cubit-wide opening at the top (*tsohar*, 6:16 — meaning debated: "window" or "roof") create a sealed, coffin-like enclosure. The ark is not steered; it has no rudder, no sail, no mast. Theologically, this emphasizes total divine control.

### Primary Sources and Scholarly Works

- **Text:** *Biblia Hebraica Stuttgartensia* (BHS), the standard critical edition of the Hebrew Bible.
- **Translations:** NRSV, NJPS, or Robert Alter, *The Five Books of Moses* (2004) for a literarily sensitive English rendering.
- **Scholarly works:**
  - Richard Elliott Friedman, *Who Wrote the Bible?* (1987; reissued 2019)
  - Joel Baden, *The Composition of the Pentateuch: Renewing the Documentary Hypothesis* (2012)
  - David Carr, *Reading the Fractures of Genesis* (1996)
  - Claus Westermann, *Genesis 1-11: A Commentary* (Continental Commentary series, 1984; Eng. trans. 1994)

## 2. Epic of Gilgamesh, Tablet XI (Utnapishtim)

### Discovery

George Smith, a self-taught Assyriologist working as a restorer at the British Museum, identified the flood narrative on Tablet XI of the Gilgamesh Epic among fragments from the library of Ashurbanipal (r. 668-627 BCE) at Nineveh. He presented his findings to the Society of Biblical Archaeology on December 3, 1872. The discovery caused a sensation because of its obvious parallels with Genesis. The *Daily Telegraph* funded Smith's expedition to Nineveh in 1873, where he found additional fragments.

### Dating

- **Standard Babylonian version (from which Tablet XI comes):** compiled/edited ca. 1200-1100 BCE, attributed to the scholar-priest Sin-leqi-unninni.
- **Old Babylonian versions** of the Gilgamesh epic: ca. 1800-1600 BCE, but these do *not* contain the flood story. The flood narrative was incorporated into the Gilgamesh cycle from the independent Atrahasis tradition at the Standard Babylonian stage or slightly earlier.
- **Ashurbanipal's library copies** (the actual tablets Smith read): 7th century BCE.

### Key Narrative Elements

- **Flood hero:** Utnapishtim ("He Found Life"), also called Ziusudra (Sumerian) or Atrahasis ("Exceedingly Wise").
- **Warning:** The god Ea (Akkadian for Sumerian Enki) warns Utnapishtim by speaking to a reed wall/reed hut, circumventing the divine oath of secrecy.
- **Vessel dimensions:** A perfect cube — 120 cubits on each side (approximately 60 meters / 200 feet). Seven decks, nine internal compartments per deck. Waterproofed with bitumen inside and out. The cubic shape is ritually/symbolically significant but hydrodynamically absurd.
- **Loading:** Family, kin, craftsmen, "all the animals of the field," gold and silver.
- **Flood duration:** Six days and seven nights of storm.
- **Landing site:** Mount Nisir (also read as Mount Nimush), identified with Pir Omar Gudrun in the Zagros Mountains of northeastern Iraq.
- **Bird sequence:** Dove sent out (returns), swallow sent out (returns), raven sent out (does not return — finds food). Note the inversion compared to Genesis, where the raven goes first.
- **Aftermath:** Utnapishtim offers a sacrifice on the mountaintop. The gods "crowded like flies around the offering." Enlil is furious that anyone survived. Ea rebukes Enlil. Enlil relents and grants Utnapishtim and his wife immortality, settling them at "the mouth of the rivers" (Dilmun).
- **No universal covenant.** The flood is not resolved with a promise to all humanity; instead, one couple is removed from the mortal world.

### Parallels and Differences with Genesis

| Feature | Gilgamesh XI | Genesis 6-9 |
|---|---|---|
| Divine motivation | Unclear (noise?); gods act collectively | Human wickedness / corruption |
| Warning method | Indirect (through reed wall) | Direct divine speech to Noah |
| Vessel shape | Cube (120 x 120 x 120 cubits) | Rectangular barge (300 x 50 x 30 cubits) |
| Passengers | Family, craftsmen, artisans | Family only (8 persons) |
| Flood duration | 6 days, 7 nights | 40 days (J) / ~1 year (P) |
| Birds | Dove, swallow, raven | Raven, dove (x3) |
| Landing site | Mt. Nisir/Nimush (Zagros) | Mountains of Ararat (eastern Turkey) |
| Sacrifice | Gods crowd "like flies" | YHWH smells "pleasing aroma" |
| Resolution | Hero given personal immortality | Universal covenant, rainbow sign |

### Primary Source and Scholarly Works

- **Critical edition:** Andrew R. George, *The Babylonian Gilgamesh Epic*, 2 vols. (Oxford University Press, 2003) — the definitive scholarly edition.
- **Accessible translation:** Andrew R. George, *The Epic of Gilgamesh* (Penguin Classics, 1999).
- **Also:** Stephanie Dalley, *Myths from Mesopotamia* (Oxford World's Classics, rev. ed. 2000).
- **Tablets:** The primary tablets are held in the British Museum (BM numbers include K.3375 and related fragments from Kuyunjik/Nineveh).

## 3. Atrahasis Epic

### Dating

- **Old Babylonian version:** The most complete early copy was written by the scribe Kasap-aya (Ipiq-Aya) during the reign of King Ammi-saduqa of Babylon, ca. **1646-1626 BCE**. This makes it significantly older than the Standard Babylonian Gilgamesh version.
- **Later copies and fragments** from Assyrian libraries (7th century BCE) supplement the Old Babylonian text.

### Key Narrative Elements

Atrahasis is not just a flood story — it is a **creation-to-flood epic** in three tablets:

- **Tablet I:** The creation of humanity. The lesser gods (Igigi) rebel against forced labor (digging canals). To resolve the crisis, Enki and the mother goddess Mami/Nintu create humans from clay mixed with the flesh and blood of a slain god (We-ilu, "a god who has intelligence") to bear the labor of the gods.

- **Tablet II:** The **noise motif** (*huburu / rigmu*). Humanity multiplies and becomes noisy, disturbing Enlil's sleep. Enlil sends plague, then drought, then famine to reduce the human population. Each time, the wise Atrahasis ("Exceedingly Wise") consults Enki, who provides a countermeasure. The divine motivation for the flood is **not moral** (as in Genesis) but **demographic/acoustic** — overpopulation and the disturbance it causes. Some scholars (e.g., Anne Kilmer) interpret "noise" metaphorically as rebellion or social disorder.

- **Tablet III:** Enlil, frustrated that his measures have failed, decrees the flood (*abubu*). Enki again warns Atrahasis by speaking through a reed wall. Atrahasis builds a boat, loads his family and animals, and survives. After the flood, the gods are starving (no humans to offer sacrifices). Enlil is furious but eventually accepts the situation. Enki and the mother goddess institute **population control measures**: infant mortality, celibate priestesses (*naditu*), and the demon Pashittu who snatches babies. This is the Atrahasis epic's distinctive resolution — not a covenant, not immortality, but demographic controls.

### Significance for Comparative Study

Atrahasis provides the **theological and narrative framework** from which the Gilgamesh XI flood was extracted and condensed. The noise-of-humanity motif is almost entirely absent from Genesis, where the motivation is ethical.

### Primary Sources and Scholarly Works

- **Critical edition:** W.G. Lambert and A.R. Millard, *Atra-hasis: The Babylonian Story of the Flood* (Oxford, 1969).
- **Translation:** Stephanie Dalley, *Myths from Mesopotamia* (Oxford World's Classics, rev. ed. 2000), pp. 1-38.
- **Key scholarship:**
  - Tikva Frymer-Kensky, "The Atrahasis Epic and Its Significance for Our Understanding of Genesis 1-9," *Biblical Archaeologist* 40 (1977): 147-155.
  - Anne Kilmer, "The Mesopotamian Concept of Overpopulation and Its Solution as Reflected in the Mythology," *Orientalia* 41 (1972): 160-177.

## 4. Eridu Genesis (Ziusudra)

### Dating

- **The surviving tablet** (CBS 10673 + Ni 10934, held at the University of Pennsylvania Museum) is a **copy dating to ca. 1600 BCE** from Nippur, written in Sumerian.
- **The composition itself** is estimated to date to the **late 3rd millennium BCE** (ca. 2100-2000 BCE), making it the **oldest known flood narrative**.
- First published by Arno Poebel in 1914.

### Key Narrative Elements

The tablet is extremely **fragmentary** — perhaps only one-third of the text survives:

- **Creation and civilization:** The gods create humanity. Kingship is lowered from heaven. Five antediluvian cities are founded: Eridu, Bad-tibira, Larak, Sippar, and Shuruppak. (These match the Sumerian King List's antediluvian cities.)
- **The flood hero:** **Ziusudra** (Sumerian: "Life of Long Days"), described as a pious, god-fearing king. He is the prototype for Atrahasis and Utnapishtim.
- **Warning:** A god (probably Enki) speaks to Ziusudra through a wall. The wall-speaking motif is already present in this oldest version.
- **The flood:** Described as a storm lasting **seven days and seven nights.**
- **Aftermath:** The sun god Utu comes forth. Ziusudra opens a window in the boat, prostrates himself before Utu. He offers animal sacrifices. Anu and Enlil grant him "life like a god" and "breath eternal like a god." He is settled in **Dilmun**.

### Primary Sources and Scholarly Works

- **Edition and translation:** Thorkild Jacobsen, "The Eridu Genesis," *Journal of Biblical Literature* 100 (1981): 513-529.
- **Context:** Thorkild Jacobsen, *The Treasures of Darkness: A History of Mesopotamian Religion* (Yale, 1976).

## 5. Berossus (*Babyloniaca*)

### Dating and Context

**Berossus** was a Babylonian priest of Marduk (Bel) who wrote the *Babyloniaca* in **Greek**, ca. **281-278 BCE**, during the reign of Antiochus I Soter. The *Babyloniaca* does **not survive intact** — known only through fragments preserved by Alexander Polyhistor (1st c. BCE), Josephus (1st c. CE), Eusebius (4th c. CE), and Moses of Chorene.

### Key Narrative Elements

- **Flood hero:** **Xisuthros**, a Greek rendering of Sumerian Ziusudra. He is the tenth antediluvian king — paralleling the ten antediluvian patriarchs of Genesis 5.
- **Divine warning:** The god Kronos (= Ea/Enki in *interpretatio Graeca*) appears to Xisuthros in a dream.
- **Vessel:** Dimensions given as approximately 5 stadia long x 2 stadia wide (enormously exaggerated, reflecting late legendary inflation).
- **Unique detail:** Xisuthros is told to bury the **tablets of knowledge** in Sippar before the flood, to be retrieved afterwards — the preservation of written knowledge.
- **Landing site:** The mountains of Armenia (consistent with the Ararat tradition).
- **Aftermath:** Xisuthros, his wife, and the pilot of the ship are taken to dwell with the gods. A voice from heaven instructs the survivors to dig up the tablets from Sippar and restore civilization.

### Significance

Berossus is the crucial **bridge** between cuneiform Mesopotamian traditions and the Greco-Roman world. His ten antediluvian kings correspond structurally to Genesis's ten antediluvian patriarchs.

### Primary Sources

- **Fragments:** Stanley Mayer Burstein, *The Babyloniaca of Berossus* (Undena, 1978).
- **Also:** Gerald P. Verbrugghe and John M. Wickersham, *Berossos and Manetho, Introduced and Translated* (University of Michigan Press, 1996).

## 6. Quran: Surah Nuh (71) and Surah Hud (11)

### Dating

The Quran was revealed ca. **610-632 CE** and compiled into its canonical form ca. **644-656 CE**. The story of Nuh (Noah) appears in multiple surahs: Nuh (71), Hud (11:25-49), Al-A'raf (7:59-64), Al-Mu'minun (23:23-30), Al-Qamar (54:9-17), Ash-Shu'ara (26:105-122), and others.

### Key Narrative Elements

- **Nuh as prophet:** One of the five greatest prophets (*Ulul-Azm*). His primary mission is to call his people away from idol worship. The Quran names specific idols: Wadd, Suwa', Yaghuth, Ya'uq, and Nasr (71:23).
- **Duration of preaching:** 950 years (29:14).
- **The son who refused:** The most dramatic divergence from Genesis. In Surah Hud 11:42-43, Nuh calls to his son (unnamed; later tradition often calls him Yam or Kan'an) who refuses to board. God replies: "O Nuh, indeed he is not of your family; indeed, he is [one whose] work was other than righteous" (11:46). Faith, not blood, defines belonging.
- **Landing site:** **Al-Judi**, not Ararat. Traditionally placed near Cizre in southeastern Turkey — different from Ararat but in the broader Armenian/Kurdish highland region.
- **No rainbow covenant.** Instead: "Descend with peace from Us and blessings upon you and upon nations [that will descend] from those with you" (11:48).

### Key Differences from Genesis

| Feature | Genesis | Quran |
|---|---|---|
| Hero's role | Righteous man, not explicitly a preacher | Prophet sent to warn his people for centuries |
| Son's fate | All sons survive (Shem, Ham, Japheth) | One son refuses and drowns |
| Landing site | Mountains of Ararat | Al-Judi |
| Aftermath covenant | Rainbow, promise never to flood again | Peace and blessings; no specific sign |
| Wife | Saved | In one tradition, Nuh's wife was among the disbelievers (66:10) |

### Primary Sources and Scholarly Works

- **Text:** The Quran, Cairo 1924 Royal Edition (standard). English: M.A.S. Abdel Haleem (Oxford, 2004).
- **Scholarly works:**
  - John C. Reeves, *Bible and Qur'an: Essays in Scriptural Intertextuality* (SBL, 2003).
  - Brannon Wheeler, *Prophets in the Quran* (Continuum, 2002).
  - Al-Tabari, *Tarikh al-Rusul wa'l-Muluk*, vol. 1 — the most important classical Islamic commentary on the Nuh story.
