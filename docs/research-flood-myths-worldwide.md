## 1. Greek -- Deucalion and Pyrrha

### Culture/Tradition
Ancient Greek (Hellenic)

### Texts and Sources

| Source | Author | Approximate Date | Key Section |
|--------|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| *Metamorphoses* | Ovid | 8 CE | Book I, lines 253--415 |
| *Bibliotheca* (*Library*) | Pseudo-Apollodorus | 1st--2nd c. CE | Book I.7.2 |
| *Works and Days* | Hesiod (fragment tradition) | c. 700 BCE | References to Deucalion lineage |
| *Timaeus* / *Critias* | Plato | c. 360 BCE | Atlantis flood (related but distinct) |
| *Argonautica* | Apollonius of Rhodes | 3rd c. BCE | References to the deluge |

### Narrative Elements

- **Divine warning:** Zeus decides to destroy the corrupt Bronze Age of humanity. In Ovid's version, he first considers fire but fears it would ignite the heavens, so he chooses flood instead. In Apollodorus, Prometheus warns his son Deucalion directly.
- **Vessel/survival method:** Deucalion builds a chest or ark (*larnax*) on his father Prometheus's advice and stocks it with provisions. He and his wife Pyrrha float for nine days and nights.
- **Animals:** Unlike the Mesopotamian/Hebrew traditions, the Greek version does not emphasize animal preservation aboard the vessel. The focus is on two human survivors.
- **Landing site:** Mount Parnassus (Ovid, *Metamorphoses* I.316--321). Some versions name Mount Othrys or Mount Etna. Apollodorus specifies Parnassus.
- **Repopulation:** After disembarking, Deucalion and Pyrrha consult the oracle of Themis, who instructs them to "throw the bones of your mother behind you." They interpret "mother" as Gaia (Earth) and "bones" as stones. The stones Deucalion throws become men; those Pyrrha throws become women. This is the famous stone-throwing repopulation motif.
- **Cause:** Human wickedness (specifically the impiety of Lycaon, who served human flesh to Zeus).
- **Scope:** Universal -- Zeus floods the entire earth, not merely a region.

### Scholarly Notes
The Deucalion myth bears structural resemblances to the Mesopotamian flood traditions (Atrahasis, Utnapishtim) but with distinctly Greek theological framing: the gods act from moral outrage rather than annoyance at human noise. The stone-throwing motif is unique to the Greek tradition and plays on the Greek etymological connection between *laos* (people) and *laas* (stone).

### Primary Source Access
- Ovid, *Metamorphoses* I.253--415: Available at [Perseus Digital Library](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu) (Latin with English translation)
- Apollodorus, *Bibliotheca* I.7.2: Available at [Theoi.com](https://www.theoi.com/Text/Apollodorus1.html) and Perseus
- Loeb Classical Library editions (Harvard University Press) for both texts

## 2. Hindu -- Matsya and Manu

### Culture/Tradition
Hindu / Vedic Indian

### Texts and Sources

| Source | Approximate Date | Key Section |
|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| *Shatapatha Brahmana* | c. 800--600 BCE | Section 1.8.1 |
| *Mahabharata* | c. 400 BCE--400 CE | Vana Parva (Book 3), chapters 185--187 |
| *Matsya Purana* | c. 250--500 CE | Chapters 1--2 |
| *Bhagavata Purana* | c. 500--1000 CE | Canto 8, chapters 24 |

### Narrative Elements

**Shatapatha Brahmana version (earliest):**
- **Divine warning:** A small fish appears in King Manu's hands while he performs the morning water ritual (*tarpana*). The fish begs Manu to protect it from larger fish, promising in return to save Manu from an impending flood.
- **Vessel/survival method:** Manu raises the fish, which grows to enormous size. When the flood comes, Manu boards a ship he has built on the fish's instructions.
- **The rope-to-horn motif:** Manu ties the ship with a rope to the horn of the great fish, who then steers the ship northward through the deluge. This is the signature motif of the Hindu flood narrative.
- **Landing site:** The fish guides the ship to the "northern mountain" (identified in later texts as the Himalayas; the Shatapatha Brahmana names it as a peak in the "northern mountains").
- **Repopulation:** Manu performs a sacrifice, pouring clarified butter and sour milk into the waters. After a year, a woman (Ida/Ila) is born from the offerings. Together, Manu and Ida become the progenitors of the human race.
- **Animals:** Not specifically preserved in the earliest version.

**Key difference -- Shatapatha Brahmana vs. later versions:**
- In the *Shatapatha Brahmana*, the fish is not identified as a deity -- it is simply a miraculous fish.
- In the *Mahabharata*, the fish is identified with Brahma (the creator god).
- In the *Matsya Purana* and *Bhagavata Purana*, the fish is explicitly Matsya, the first avatar of Vishnu. This is the version most widely known today.

**Matsya Purana version:**
- The fish reveals itself as Vishnu and warns of a cosmic dissolution (*pralaya*) that will come in seven days.
- Manu is instructed to gather the seven sages (*saptarishi*), seeds of all plants, and pairs of animals aboard a great boat.
- The serpent Vasuki (or Adi-Shesha) is used as the rope to tie the ship to the fish's horn.
- After the flood, Vishnu slays the demon Hayagriva who had stolen the Vedas, restoring sacred knowledge to the world.

### Scholarly Notes
The Shatapatha Brahmana version (c. 800--600 BCE) is notably earlier than the Genesis J-source flood narrative as dated by most documentary hypothesis scholars (c. 950--850 BCE for J), making the question of diffusion vs. independent origin a matter of genuine scholarly debate. The rope-to-horn motif has no clear parallel in Mesopotamian versions and appears to be an independent Indian development. The progressive theologization of the fish -- from anonymous creature to Brahma to Vishnu-avatar -- is a textbook case of mythological accretion across centuries of textual transmission.

### Primary Source Access
- *Shatapatha Brahmana* 1.8.1: Available at [Sacred-Texts.com](https://www.sacred-texts.com/hin/sbr/sbe12/sbe1234.htm) (Julius Eggeling translation, Sacred Books of the East series, 1882)
- *Mahabharata* Vana Parva 185--187: Available at [Sacred-Texts.com](https://www.sacred-texts.com/hin/m03/index.htm) (Kisari Mohan Ganguli translation)
- *Matsya Purana*: Partial translations available through various academic publishers; no complete standard English translation widely available online
- *Bhagavata Purana* 8.24: Available at [Vedabase.io](https://vedabase.io/en/library/sb/8/24/)

## 3. Chinese -- The Great Flood of Gun-Yu

### Culture/Tradition
Ancient Chinese (pre-Imperial and early Imperial)

### Texts and Sources

| Source | Approximate Date | Key Section |
|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| *Shangshu* (*Book of Documents*) | Core texts c. 6th--5th c. BCE; compilation later | "Canon of Yao" (*Yao Dian*) |
| *Shanhaijing* (*Classic of Mountains and Seas*) | c. 4th c. BCE--1st c. CE | Various sections on Gun |
| *Mengzi* (*Mencius*) | c. 300 BCE | Book 3A.4 |
| *Shiji* (*Records of the Grand Historian*) | c. 94 BCE (Sima Qian) | "Basic Annals of the Xia" |
| *Huainanzi* | c. 139 BCE | Various flood references |
| *Lushi Chunqiu* | c. 239 BCE | Various flood references |

### Narrative Elements

- **The flood:** During the reign of Emperor Yao, a catastrophic flood inundates China. The *Shangshu* describes how "the inundating waters seemed to assail the heavens" and "the vast waters were obstructive, embracing the mountains and overtopping the hills."
- **Gun's failure (engineering approach #1):** Emperor Yao appoints Gun to control the flood. Gun attempts to block the waters using a self-expanding magical soil called *xirang* (breathing earth), stolen from the supreme god. After nine years of failure, Gun is executed (or exiled) by Emperor Shun (Yao's successor). In some versions (e.g., *Shanhaijing*), Gun is killed and his body does not decay; Yu is born from his belly.
- **Yu the Great's success (engineering approach #2):** Yu, Gun's son, is appointed to continue the work. Rather than damming/blocking the water (his father's approach), Yu dredges channels and directs the water to the sea. He labors for thirteen years, passing his own home three times without entering. Yu succeeds and is rewarded with the throne, founding the Xia dynasty.
- **Unique Chinese approach -- no ark:** This is the critical distinction from virtually all other flood traditions worldwide. The Chinese Great Flood is solved not through divine rescue or an ark but through *human engineering and labor*. There is no vessel, no divine warning to build a boat, no animals taken aboard. The flood is a problem to be solved by human ingenuity and perseverance. This reflects core Chinese philosophical values: the primacy of human effort, hydraulic engineering as the foundation of civilization, and the mandate earned through meritorious service.
- **Divine/supernatural elements:** While the narrative is more "historical" than mythological compared to other traditions, supernatural elements persist: Gun's theft of *xirang*, a dragon that drags its tail to carve channels for Yu, Yu's transformation into a bear while digging through a mountain, and a divine tortoise that carries the *xirang*.

### The 2016 *Science* Paper: Possible Historical Basis

Wu Qinglong et al., "Outburst flood at 1920 BCE supports historicity of China's Great Flood and the Xia dynasty," *Science* 353, no. 6299 (5 August 2016): 579--582. DOI: [10.1126/science.aaf0842](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aaf0842)

**Key findings:**
- An earthquake triggered a massive landslide that dammed the Yellow River at Jishi Gorge in Qinghai Province, creating a lake that rose approximately 800 feet (245 m) above the current river level.
- When the dam breached, it released approximately 3.8 cubic miles (15.8 km^3) of water -- one of the largest known freshwater floods of the Holocene.
- Radiocarbon dating (including of human remains from the earthquake) places this event at approximately 1920 BCE.
- The flood would have devastated downstream areas of the Yellow River valley for years, potentially corresponding to the legendary Great Flood.
- This dating would place the founding of the Xia dynasty at approximately 1900 BCE -- several centuries later than traditional Chinese chronology suggests, but coinciding with the archaeological transition from Neolithic to Bronze Age cultures in the region (the Erlitou culture).

**Scholarly debate:** The paper generated significant responses in *Science*, including comments questioning the sedimentological interpretation and the magnitude estimates. The connection between this geological event and the mythological Great Flood remains contested but represents the strongest candidate for a historical kernel behind any flood tradition worldwide.

### Primary Source Access
- *Shangshu* ("Canon of Yao"): James Legge translation at [CText.org](https://ctext.org/shang-shu/canon-of-yao) (Chinese Text Project, with original Chinese)
- Sima Qian, *Shiji* "Basic Annals of the Xia": Burton Watson translation (Columbia University Press); also at [CText.org](https://ctext.org/shiji)
- Wu et al. 2016: [Science](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aaf0842) (paywall); summary at [ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2016/08/160804152433.htm)

## 4. Mesoamerican

### 4a. Maya -- Popol Vuh

### Culture/Tradition
K'iche' Maya (Highland Guatemala)

### Texts and Sources

| Source | Approximate Date | Notes |
|--------|-------------------|-------|
| *Popol Vuh* (colonial manuscript) | Written c. 1554--1558 CE | Transcription of older oral tradition; surviving copy is Francisco Ximenez's c. 1701 transcription |
| Pre-Columbian oral tradition | Pre-contact origins (dating uncertain) | The *Popol Vuh* records traditions of much greater antiquity |

### Narrative Elements

- **Context:** The flood occurs during the creation narrative, specifically the destruction of the second creation (the "wooden people").
- **The wooden people:** The gods (Heart of Sky, Hunahpu, and Xbalanque's divine predecessors) create humans from wood after a failed attempt with mud. The wooden people can speak and multiply but have no hearts, no minds, no memory of their creators. They do not worship the gods.
- **Destruction by flood:** The gods send a great flood to destroy the wooden people. A rain of resin falls from the sky. The wooden people are attacked by their own possessions -- their grinding stones, cooking pots, dogs, and turkeys turn against them. Those who survive the flood are transformed into monkeys (explaining the existence of spider monkeys and howler monkeys as degraded remnants of a failed humanity).
- **Unique elements:** This flood is not a punishment for moral evil (as in Greek or Hebrew traditions) but a *design correction* -- the creators are refining their creation through iteration. The flood is one step in a progressive creation narrative. The animate revolt of objects is unique to the Maya version.
- **No ark or vessel:** There is no survival vessel, no warning to a righteous individual, no animal preservation. The wooden people are simply destroyed (with monkey survivors as remnants, not as saved beings).

### Scholarly Notes
The *Popol Vuh* flood does not map neatly onto the "standard" flood myth template because it functions within a cycle of creation-destruction-recreation. Dennis Tedlock's authoritative translation (1985, revised 1996) and Allen Christenson's more recent translation (2007) are the standard scholarly editions.

### Primary Source Access
- *Popol Vuh*: Dennis Tedlock translation (Simon & Schuster, 1985/1996); Allen Christenson translation (University of Oklahoma Press, 2007)
- Ximenez manuscript held at the Newberry Library, Chicago
- Christenson's translation freely available: [Mesoweb](http://www.mesoweb.com/publications/Christenson/PopolVuh.pdf)

### 4b. Aztec -- Five Suns Cosmology (Fourth Sun / Nahui Atl)

### Culture/Tradition
Aztec / Mexica (Central Mexico, Nahua peoples)

### Texts and Sources

| Source | Approximate Date | Notes |
|--------|-------------------|-------|
| *Leyenda de los Soles* ("Legend of the Suns") | c. 1558 CE (Nahuatl text) | Codex Chimalpopoca |
| *Historia de los Mexicanos por sus Pinturas* | c. 1535 CE | Spanish colonial account |
| *Histoyre du Mechique* | c. 1543 CE | French translation of lost Spanish original |
| Sun Stone (Aztec Calendar Stone) | Late 15th c. CE | Carved monumental record of the Five Suns |

### Narrative Elements

- **Cosmological framework:** Aztec cosmology posits five successive world-ages (Suns), each ruled by a different deity and destroyed by a different element. The Fourth Sun, *Nahui Atl* ("Four Water"), ends in flood.
- **The Fourth Sun:** Governed by Chalchiuhtlicue, goddess of lakes and streams (Tlaloc's consort or sister, depending on the source). According to the *Leyenda de los Soles*, this sun lasted 676 years.
- **The flood:** Tezcatlipoca accuses Chalchiuhtlicue of false piety, claiming she only provides water to appear generous. Wounded by this accusation, Chalchiuhtlicue weeps tears of blood for 52 years, causing catastrophic flooding that engulfs the world. The sky collapses. The inhabitants of this world are transformed into fish.
- **Survival of Tata and Nene:** In the *Leyenda de los Soles*, the god Tezcatlipoca (or in some versions, Titlacahuan) warns a man named Tata and his wife Nene to hollow out a great log (*ahuahuetl*, cypress tree) and hide inside it. They are instructed to eat only one ear of maize each until the waters recede.
- **Post-flood transgression:** After emerging, Tata and Nene catch fish and roast them. The gods (or the stars) smell the smoke and become angry. Tezcatlipoca descends, beheads them, and attaches their heads to their backsides, transforming them into dogs. (This punitive transformation differs markedly from the Abrahamic tradition of post-flood covenant.)
- **The current era:** We live in the Fifth Sun (*Nahui Ollin*, "Four Movement"), destined to end in earthquakes.

### Scholarly Notes
The Five Suns framework demonstrates that Mesoamerican flood traditions are embedded within cyclical cosmologies of destruction and recreation, fundamentally different from the linear narratives of Western Asian traditions. The transformation of flood survivors into dogs as punishment is a unique motif.

### Primary Source Access
- *Leyenda de los Soles*: John Bierhorst translation, *History and Mythology of the Aztecs: The Codex Chimalpopoca* (University of Arizona Press, 1992)
- *Historia de los Mexicanos por sus Pinturas*: In Angel Maria Garibay K., *Teogonia e historia de los mexicanos* (Mexico: Porrua, 1965)
- The Sun Stone is held at the Museo Nacional de Antropologia, Mexico City

## 5. South American

### 5a. Inca -- Viracocha's Flood (Unu Pachakuti)

### Culture/Tradition
Inca / Andean (Quechua-speaking peoples)

### Texts and Sources

| Source | Author | Approximate Date |
|--------|--------|-------------------|
| *Relacion de Antiguedades deste Reyno del Peru* | Juan de Santa Cruz Pachacuti Yamqui | c. 1613 |
| *Comentarios Reales de los Incas* | Inca Garcilaso de la Vega | 1609 |
| *Suma y narracion de los Incas* | Juan de Betanzos | 1551 |
| *Cronica del Peru* | Pedro Cieza de Leon | 1553 |
| Various oral traditions collected by Spanish chroniclers | Multiple | 16th--17th c. CE |

### Narrative Elements

- **The flood -- Unu Pachakuti:** "Unu" = water; "Pachakuti" = world-reversal or cataclysm. Viracocha, the creator god, first creates a race of giants from stone, but they displease him (they are brainless, or disobedient, depending on the source). He destroys them with a great flood lasting 60 days and 60 nights.
- **Location:** Lake Titicaca is central. Viracocha emerges from the waters of Titicaca, creates the sun, moon, and stars from islands in the lake, and from the lake region begins the post-flood recreation.
- **Survival:** In some versions, a small number of people survive by hiding in caves in high mountains; in others, Viracocha specifically preserves two individuals to bring civilization to the world.
- **Repopulation:** Viracocha creates new, better humans from smaller stones (an echo of the Greek stone-to-people motif, though almost certainly independent). He breathes life into them and sends them underground, to emerge from caves, springs, and lakes (*pacarinas*) across the Andes.
- **Cyclical cosmology:** Like the Mesoamerican traditions, the Inca flood is one episode in a cycle of world-ages, each ending in destruction and followed by recreation.

### Primary Source Access
- Betanzos, *Suma y narracion de los Incas*: Roland Hamilton and Dana Buchanan translation (University of Texas Press, 1996)
- Garcilaso de la Vega, *Royal Commentaries of the Incas*: Harold Livermore translation (University of Texas Press, 1966)
- Cieza de Leon: Alexandra Parma Cook and Noble David Cook translation (Duke University Press, 1998)

### 5b. Mapuche -- Trentren Vilu and Caicai Vilu

### Culture/Tradition
Mapuche (south-central Chile and southwestern Argentina)

### Texts and Sources
Primarily oral tradition, recorded by Spanish missionaries and later ethnographers. Key compilations include:
- Tomas Guevara, *Historia de la civilizacion de Araucania* (1898--1902)
- Bertha Koessler-Ilg, *Tradiciones araucanas* (1954)
- Various 20th-century ethnographic collections

### Narrative Elements

- **Two cosmic serpents:** Caicai Vilu (Caicai = "related to water"; Vilu = "snake") is a sea serpent that causes floods. Trentren Vilu (Trentren = "related to earth") is a land serpent that protects humanity.
- **Cause:** Caicai Vilu awakens from a long slumber and becomes enraged at humanity's ingratitude for the sea's gifts. He strikes the water with his fish-shaped tail, commanding the waters to flood the valleys and hills.
- **Survival method:** Trentren Vilu raises the mountains to save the people and animals. Humans and animals climb onto Trentren's back and are carried to higher ground. Those trapped by the rising waters are transformed by Trentren into birds, fish, or marine mammals so they can survive.
- **Outcome:** Neither serpent fully wins. Caicai cannot flood all the land, but the waters never fully recede -- this explains Chile's current geography, with its extensive coastline, fjords, and islands. Trentren continues to manifest through earthquakes and volcanic eruptions; Caicai causes tidal waves and floods when he stirs in sleep.
- **Geological resonance:** Scholars have noted that the Mapuche homeland is one of the most seismically and volcanically active regions on Earth, and the myth encodes genuine geological realities (tsunamis, tectonic uplift, volcanism).

### Primary Source Access
- Oral tradition; see ethnographic compilations above
- English summary available in John Bierhorst, *The Mythology of South America* (William Morrow, 1988)

### 5c. Amazonian Traditions

Numerous Amazonian peoples preserve flood traditions, though the documentation is fragmentary due to the oral nature of these cultures and the destruction caused by colonization. Notable examples include:

- **Jivaro (Shuar):** A flood sent by the creator to punish humanity; a man and woman survive on a mountain and repopulate the earth.
- **Yamana (Tierra del Fuego):** A woman called Moon raises the sea in anger, causing a great flood; only those who reach the mountain peaks survive.
- **Ge-speaking peoples (Brazil):** Various flood narratives involving transformation of survivors into animals.
- **Tupi-Guarani:** Destruction of the first world by flood; Monan, the creator, destroys his creation by fire and flood.

### Primary Source Access
- Claude Levi-Strauss, *Mythologiques* (4 vols., 1964--1971) -- extensive analysis of South American myths, including flood narratives
- Jonathan Hill, ed., *Rethinking History and Myth: Indigenous South American Perspectives on the Past* (University of Illinois Press, 1988)

## 6. North American Indigenous

### 6a. Ojibwe/Anishinaabe -- Nanabozho and the Great Flood

### Culture/Tradition
Ojibwe / Anishinaabe (Great Lakes region, North America)

### Sources
Oral tradition, recorded by multiple ethnographers:
- Henry Rowe Schoolcraft, *Algic Researches* (1839)
- William Jones, *Ojibwa Texts* (Publications of the American Ethnological Society, 1917--1919)
- Basil Johnston, *Ojibway Heritage* (1976) and *The Manitous* (1995)

### Narrative Elements

- **Cause:** In some versions, Nanabozho (also Nanabush, Wenabozho, Waynaboozhoo) disobeys the Creator, leading to flood as punishment; in others, malevolent underwater spirits (the Great Lynx or underwater panthers) cause the flood after Nanabozho kills one of their leaders.
- **The flood:** Water covers the entire earth. Nanabozho survives by floating on a great log.
- **Earth-diver motif:** This is the defining structural element. Nanabozho asks various animals to dive to the bottom of the floodwaters and retrieve a handful of earth. The loon, the beaver, and the otter each try and fail (some die in the attempt). Finally, the muskrat (the smallest and least powerful) dives and is gone for a very long time. The muskrat surfaces dead or nearly dead, but clutched in its tiny paw is a handful of mud from the bottom.
- **Creation of Turtle Island:** Nanabozho takes the mud and places it on the back of a willing turtle. The wind blows from the four directions, and the mud grows and grows until it becomes the new earth -- Turtle Island (the Anishinaabe name for North America).
- **Significance of the muskrat:** The muskrat's sacrifice is a central moral lesson: the smallest and humblest creature succeeds where the strong fail. This is not merely a flood narrative but a creation narrative -- the flood is the necessary precondition for the (re)creation of the world.

### Classification
This narrative belongs to the "earth-diver" type (Stith Thompson Motif A812), one of the most widespread myth types in North America, found across dozens of indigenous cultures from the sub-Arctic to the Southwest.

### Primary Source Access
- Basil Johnston, *Ojibway Heritage* (University of Nebraska Press, 1976) -- the most accessible and culturally authorized account
- Henry Rowe Schoolcraft, *Algic Researches* (1839) -- available on Internet Archive
- Theresa Smith, *The Island of the Anishinaabeg* (University of Idaho Press, 1995) -- scholarly analysis

### 6b. Hopi -- Multiple World Destructions

### Culture/Tradition
Hopi (Northeastern Arizona)

### Sources
- Frank Waters and Oswald White Bear Fredericks, *Book of the Hopi* (1963) -- controversial but widely cited
- Harold Courlander, *The Fourth World of the Hopis* (1971) -- drawn from oral tradition
- Various clan histories maintained by Hopi elders

### Narrative Elements

- **Four Worlds framework:** The Hopi believe humanity has passed through three previous worlds, each destroyed because of human corruption and departure from the Creator's path.
  - First World: Destroyed by fire (volcanism, meteors)
  - Second World: Destroyed by ice (glaciation)
  - **Third World: Destroyed by water -- the Great Flood**
  - Fourth World: Our current world; prophesied to be destroyed by unknown means
- **The Third World flood:** Sotuknang (nephew of the Creator, Taiowa) destroys the Third World with a great deluge.
- **Survival method:** Spider Woman (*Kokyangwuti*) saves the righteous people by sealing them inside hollow reeds (or bamboo stalks), which float on the floodwaters like boats. This is a striking parallel to the Aztec hollow-log motif.
- **Emergence:** When the waters recede, the people emerge from the reeds into the Fourth World through the *sipapu* -- a hole connecting the worlds. Every Hopi kiva (underground ceremonial chamber) contains a small hole in the floor representing the sipapu, a permanent architectural reminder of this emergence.
- **Migration:** After emerging, the people are told to migrate in all four directions before settling in the center (the Hopi mesas). The flood is thus the precondition for the Hopi's sacred geography.

### Scholarly Notes
The Hopi tradition is remarkable for embedding the flood within a multi-stage cyclical cosmology that includes fire, ice, and flood destructions -- a sequence that, coincidentally or not, mirrors the actual geological history of the Pleistocene-Holocene transition.

### Primary Source Access
- Harold Courlander, *The Fourth World of the Hopis* (University of New Mexico Press, 1971) -- considered more culturally sensitive than Waters
- Ekkehart Malotki and Michael Lomatuway'ma, *Maasaw: Profile of a Hopi God* (University of Nebraska Press, 1987)
- Note: Many Hopi elders have objected to the publication of sacred narratives; researchers should approach this tradition with cultural sensitivity

### 6c. Cree -- Wisakedjak and the Great Flood

### Culture/Tradition
Cree (sub-Arctic and boreal Canada)

### Sources
Oral tradition, recorded by:
- Alanson Skinner, *Notes on the Eastern Cree and Northern Saulteaux* (1911)
- Various 20th-century ethnographic recordings
- Elder-led educational materials

### Narrative Elements

- **Cause:** Wisakedjak (also Wìsakedjàk, Wīsahkēcāhk), the Cree trickster figure, disobeys the Creator. The Creator had tasked Wisakedjak with keeping animals and humans from quarreling, but he fails. The Creator floods the world to begin life anew.
- **The flood:** Water covers the entire earth. Wisakedjak floats on a raft or log.
- **Earth-diver sequence:** Wisakedjak sends Otter to dive for earth -- Otter fails. Beaver dives -- Beaver fails. Finally Muskrat dives and is gone the longest. Muskrat returns with a small piece of earth (or dies with earth in its paw, as in the Ojibwe version).
- **Recreation:** Wisakedjak places the earth on a willing creature's back (often a turtle) and blows on it from all directions, causing it to grow into the new land.
- **Structural parallel:** The Cree version is closely cognate with the Ojibwe Nanabozho narrative, reflecting their shared Algonquian linguistic and cultural heritage. The trickster figure (Wisakedjak/Nanabozho) and the earth-diver motif are the consistent structural elements across the Algonquian culture area.

### Primary Source Access
- Robert Brightman, *Grateful Prey: Rock Cree Human-Animal Relationships* (University of California Press, 1993)
- Various recordings at the Canadian Museum of History and provincial archives

### 6d. Pacific Northwest Traditions

### Culture/Tradition
Multiple nations: Tlingit, Haida, Kwakwaka'wakw, Squamish, others

### Sources
- Franz Boas, *Tsimshian Mythology* (1916) and *Kwakiutl Ethnography* (1966)
- John Swanton, *Tlingit Myths and Texts* (Bureau of American Ethnology, 1909)
- CEETEP workshop materials on tsunamis and floods in Native American oral tradition (Oregon State University)

### Narrative Elements

**Tlingit (southeastern Alaska):**
- Raven (or the Raven-at-the-head-of-Nass) causes a great flood, sometimes by wearing a magical hat from which water pours.
- The flood covers all the land except the highest mountain peaks.
- People survive by tying their canoes together and to the mountaintops.
- After the waters recede, the canoes come to rest and people repopulate from the survivors.

**General Pacific Northwest patterns:**
- Flood narratives are widespread throughout the region, with many possibly encoding memory of actual tsunamis (the Cascadia subduction zone produces catastrophic tsunamis on a roughly 300--500-year recurrence interval; the last major event was January 26, 1700).
- Canoe-based survival is a common element (reflecting the maritime culture of these peoples).
- Mountain-peak refuge is nearly universal.
- Transformation of people into animals or landscape features is common in post-flood narratives.

### Scholarly Notes
Ruth Ludwin et al., "Dating the 1700 Cascadia Earthquake: Great Coastal Earthquakes in Native Stories," *Seismological Research Letters* 76, no. 2 (2005): 140--148, demonstrates that Pacific Northwest indigenous oral traditions preserve accurate memories of specific historical seismic and tsunami events, sometimes datable to specific occurrences.

## 7. Australian Aboriginal

### Culture/Tradition
Multiple Aboriginal Australian nations across the continent

### Sources
- Patrick D. Nunn and Nicholas J. Reid, "Aboriginal Memories of Inundation of the Australian Coast Dating from More than 7000 Years Ago," *Australian Geographer* 47, no. 1 (2016): 11--47. DOI: [10.1080/00049182.2015.1077539](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/00049182.2015.1077539)
- Patrick Nunn, *The Edge of Memory: Ancient Stories, Oral Tradition and the Post-Glacial World* (Bloomsbury, 2018)
- Various ethnographic collections from the 19th and 20th centuries
- R.M. and C.H. Berndt, *The World of the First Australians* (1964)

### Narrative Elements

**General patterns in Dreamtime flood narratives:**
- Coastal Aboriginal groups across Australia preserve stories of a time when the sea was lower and land extended far beyond the present coastline, and of the sea rising to cover that land.
- These stories are embedded within the Dreamtime (*Tjukurpa*, *Jukurrpa*, or other language-specific terms), the sacred narrative framework of Aboriginal cosmology.
- Unlike most other flood traditions, these are often *not* punishment narratives. They describe landscape change, sometimes attributed to ancestral beings, sometimes presented as observed fact transmitted through oral tradition.

**Specific examples:**
- **Port Phillip Bay (Victoria):** Stories describe a time when the bay was dry land with a river running through it, and of the sea rushing in to fill it.
- **Spencer Gulf (South Australia):** Narratives of the sea flooding a previously dry plain.
- **Kangaroo Island (South Australia):** Stories of a land bridge connecting the island to the mainland, destroyed by rising seas.
- **Great Barrier Reef coast (Queensland):** Stories of a time when the reef was dry land and the coast extended much farther seaward.

### The Remarkable Claim: 7,000+ Year Memory

**Nunn and Reid's research (2016):**
- Collected 21 groups of stories from locations around the entire Australian coastline describing the inundation of formerly dry coastal land by the sea.
- Using sea-level reconstructions and bathymetric data, they demonstrated that these stories consistently describe geographic conditions that last existed when sea levels were lower -- i.e., before approximately 7,000 years ago (and in some cases, up to 10,000--13,000 years ago).
- The huge geographic distances between the storytelling communities (spanning the entire continent) and the unique local details in each story make it extremely unlikely these derive from a single invented source.
- **Dating range:** The oral traditions appear to encode memories from approximately 7,250 to 13,070 calibrated years before present.

**Nunn's *The Edge of Memory* (2018):**
- Extends the analysis globally, arguing that many indigenous oral traditions preserve genuine memories of post-Ice Age environmental change.
- Examines the cultural mechanisms that enable such extraordinarily long-lived oral transmission: conservative, law-based storytelling traditions; kin-based systems for tracking knowledge accuracy; severe penalties for altering sacred narratives; and regular ritual performance that reinforces transmission fidelity.

### Scholarly Significance
If Nunn and Reid's interpretation is correct, Australian Aboriginal flood traditions represent the oldest accurately dated oral traditions in the world, preserving memories of real environmental events across more than 7,000 years of unbroken oral transmission -- possibly more than 10,000 years. This has profound implications for our understanding of oral tradition as a historical source.

### Primary Source Access
- Nunn and Reid (2016): Available through [Taylor & Francis / Australian Geographer](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/00049182.2015.1077539)
- Nunn, *The Edge of Memory* (Bloomsbury, 2018): Widely available in print
- [The Conversation article by Nunn and Reid](https://theconversation.com/ancient-aboriginal-stories-preserve-history-of-a-rise-in-sea-level-36010) (accessible summary)
- [Scientific American summary](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/ancient-sea-rise-tale-told-accurately-for-10-000-years/)

## 8. Polynesian

### Culture/Tradition
Multiple Polynesian peoples (Hawaiian, Tahitian, Maori, Samoan, and others)

### Sources
- Abraham Fornander, *An Account of the Polynesian Race* (3 vols., 1878--1885) and *Fornander Collection of Hawaiian Antiquities and Folk-lore* (1916--1920)
- Martha Beckwith, *Hawaiian Mythology* (Yale University Press, 1940)
- Various missionary and ethnographic accounts (19th century)
- Buckova, "Deluge in Polynesian Mythology," *Asian and African Studies* 13 (2004): 191--197

### Narrative Elements

**Hawaiian -- Nu'u:**
- In Hawaiian tradition, Nu'u is the figure analogous to Noah. He builds a great canoe (or ark) and survives a flood that covers all land except the peak of Mauna Kea on the Big Island of Hawai'i.
- After the flood, Nu'u mistakenly offers sacrifices to the moon, attributing his salvation to it. Kane (the creator god) descends on a rainbow and corrects Nu'u's error.
- **Caution on missionary influence:** Scholars have debated whether the Nu'u story is genuinely pre-contact or influenced by Christian missionaries who arrived in Hawai'i in the 1820s. The name "Nu'u" and the narrative's close structural parallel to Noah have raised suspicions. Fornander (19th c.) argued for indigenous origin; modern scholars are more cautious.

**Tahitian -- Ruahatu:**
- The sea god Ruahatu is offended when fishermen's hooks snag his hair. He causes the sea to rise and flood all the land.
- A man named Rata (or, in some versions, a couple) is warned and escapes to the highest mountain.
- Only mountaintops remain above water.

**General Polynesian patterns:**
- The Polynesian god Taaroa (Tangaroa), angry at human disobedience, overturns the world into the sea; only mountain peaks (*aurus*) remain as islands.
- Sea-level rise, rather than rain, is typically the mechanism of flooding -- consistent with the maritime cosmology of Pacific peoples.
- Mountaintop survival is universal in Polynesian flood traditions.
- Canoe construction as survival method (reflecting the centrality of canoe-building to Polynesian culture).

### Scholarly Notes
The question of pre-contact vs. missionary-influenced content is a persistent methodological challenge for Polynesian flood myths. Oxford Reference notes that "the biblical flood story has been entwined with both Melanesian and Polynesian legend, but there is no reason to suppose that independent stories did not exist prior to the arrival of missionaries." Patrick Nunn's work on Pacific island geology and mythology (*The Geography of Pacific Island Origin Myths*, 2003) has shown that some Pacific flood traditions may encode genuine memories of volcanic island subsidence and coastal inundation.

### Primary Source Access
- Beckwith, *Hawaiian Mythology* (1940): Available at [Sacred-Texts.com](https://sacred-texts.com/pac/hm/index.htm)
- Fornander, *Fornander Collection*: Available on [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/74570/pg74570-images.html) and Internet Archive
- Patrick Nunn, "The Geography of Pacific Island Origin Myths," available via [University of Hawaii HIGP](https://www.higp.hawaii.edu/~scott/GG104/Readings/Nunn_2003.pdf)

## 9. African

### 9a. Yoruba -- Olokun's Flood

### Culture/Tradition
Yoruba (southwestern Nigeria, Benin)

### Sources
- E. Bolaji Idowu, *Olodumare: God in Yoruba Belief* (1962)
- Oral tradition, recorded by various ethnographers and missionaries
- Harold Courlander, *Tales of Yoruba Gods and Heroes* (1973)

### Narrative Elements

- **Creation context:** In the beginning, Olorun (the supreme sky god) rules the sky, and Olokun (goddess/god of the sea; gender varies by tradition) rules the watery domain below.
- **Cause:** Obatala, on Olorun's behalf, descends from the sky and creates dry land in Olokun's domain, forming the earth. Olokun is not consulted and is enraged at the usurpation of her territory.
- **The flood:** When Obatala returns to the sky, Olokun summons the great waves and sends them surging across the land. Wave after wave floods the earth, drowning many people.
- **Resolution:** Orunmila (the god of divination) descends on the golden chain to earth and casts spells that cause the floodwaters to retreat. Olokun then challenges Olorun to a weaving contest; a chameleon duplicates every fabric she produces, demonstrating Olorun's superior power. Olokun concedes.
- **No ark or vessel:** There is no survival vessel. The flood is resolved through divine intervention (magical recession of waters), not human engineering or boat-building.

### Scholarly Notes
African flood myths are less well-documented in the comparative literature than those from other continents, partly due to the oral nature of transmission and the relative late arrival of systematic ethnographic collection. However, flood traditions have been identified among the Yoruba, the Kwaya and other peoples around Lake Victoria, the Maasai, and various West African groups. The continent does *not* have the density of flood traditions found in the Americas or South/Southeast Asia.

### 9b. Egyptian

Ancient Egyptian civilization, despite its dependence on the Nile flood cycle, does not have a prominent "universal flood" myth comparable to the Mesopotamian or Greek traditions. However:

- **The Book of the Heavenly Cow** (found in the tomb of Tutankhamun and other New Kingdom tombs, c. 1325 BCE): Ra attempts to destroy rebellious humanity by sending the goddess Hathor/Sekhmet to slaughter them. He then floods the fields with beer dyed to look like blood, which Sekhmet drinks and becomes too intoxicated to continue the slaughter. This is a *destruction of humanity* narrative with a *flooding* element, but the flood is of beer, not water, and serves to *save* humanity, not destroy it.
- The annual Nile inundation was central to Egyptian cosmology but was framed as a beneficial, cyclical event (the god Hapi), not a catastrophic destruction.

### Primary Source Access
- Idowu, *Olodumare: God in Yoruba Belief* (Longmans, 1962)
- Courlander, *Tales of Yoruba Gods and Heroes* (Crown Publishers, 1973)
- Book of the Heavenly Cow: Translation in Erik Hornung, *The Ancient Egyptian Books of the Afterlife* (Cornell University Press, 1999)

## 10. Norse

### Culture/Tradition
Norse / Old Scandinavian (Iceland, Scandinavia)

### Texts and Sources

| Source | Author | Approximate Date |
|--------|--------|-------------------|
| *Prose Edda* (*Gylfaginning*) | Snorri Sturluson | c. 1220 CE |
| *Poetic Edda* (*Grimnismal*, *Vafthrudnismal*) | Anonymous | Composed orally earlier; manuscript c. 1270 CE |

### Narrative Elements

- **Ymir's blood flood:** When the three gods Odin, Vili, and Ve slay the primordial frost giant Ymir and use his body to create the world, the blood pouring from Ymir's wounds creates an enormous flood that drowns all the frost giants (*jotnar*).
- **Bergelmir's survival:** One frost giant, Bergelmir, and his wife survive by climbing into a *ludr* (variously translated as a hollowed log, a coffin, a flour-bin, or a boat -- the Old Norse word is ambiguous). They float on the blood-sea and survive to repopulate the frost giant race.
- **This is a creation-flood, not a punishment-flood:** The flood of Ymir's blood is a byproduct of cosmogonic violence (the killing of the primordial being to create the world), not a divine punishment for human wickedness. It is structurally closer to the Babylonian Tiamat narrative (primordial being slain; body becomes the world) than to the Noah/Utnapishtim tradition.
- **Ragnarok and water:** The apocalyptic destruction of Ragnarok does involve the sea surging over the land (when the World Serpent Jormungandr releases its grip on its tail and thrashes in the ocean), but this is part of a comprehensive destruction (fire from Surtr, collapse of the sky, etc.), not specifically a "flood" myth.

### Scholarly Notes on Christian Influence
Scholars John Lindow and Carolyne Larrington have both suggested that Snorri Sturluson's account of Bergelmir's survival on a *ludr* may have been influenced by the biblical Noah narrative, given that Snorri was a Christian writing in 13th-century Iceland. The motif of a single family surviving a universal flood on a floating vessel is suspiciously close to the Genesis account. However, the Ymir-slaying itself is clearly pre-Christian and appears in the older *Poetic Edda* poems (*Vafthrudnismal* 21, *Grimnismal* 40--41).

### Primary Source Access
- *Prose Edda* (*Gylfaginning*): Arthur Gilchrist Brodeur translation (1916), available at [Mythopedia](https://mythopedia.com/library/prose-edda-brodeur-1916/gylfaginning/) and [Sacred-Texts.com](https://sacred-texts.com/neu/pre/)
- *Poetic Edda*: Carolyne Larrington translation (Oxford World's Classics, revised 2014); Henry Adams Bellows translation (1936) at [Sacred-Texts.com](https://sacred-texts.com/neu/poe/)
- Jesse Byock translation of the *Prose Edda* (Penguin Classics, 2005) -- most accessible modern edition

## 11. Comparative Mythology and Scholarship

### How Many Cultures Have Flood Narratives?

The commonly cited figure is **200--500+** distinct flood traditions worldwide, though this number depends heavily on methodology and definition:

- **Conservative estimates:** ~200 identifiable distinct flood narratives across cultures (Mark Isaac, *The Counter-Creationism Handbook*, 2007; talkorigins.org flood myth compilation).
- **Higher estimates:** Robert Schoch has cited "at least 500 flood myths." Various compilations go higher, though often counting variants of the same tradition as separate entries.
- **Important caveat:** While flood myths are widespread, they are *not* universal. Many cultures have no flood traditions at all. Sub-Saharan Africa, in particular, has relatively few flood myths compared to the Americas, South/Southeast Asia, and the Pacific. The claim that "every culture has a flood myth" is an exaggeration.
- **Geographic distribution:** Flood myths are densest in: (1) the Americas (both North and South), (2) South and Southeast Asia, (3) Oceania/Pacific, (4) the ancient Near East and Mediterranean. They are sparser in: (1) Sub-Saharan Africa, (2) Central and East Asia (the Chinese tradition being the notable exception).

### Stith Thompson's Motif-Index Classification

Stith Thompson, *Motif-Index of Folk-Literature* (6 vols., 1932--1936; revised and expanded 1955--1958):

- **A1010:** "Deluge. Inundation of whole world or section." This is the master motif for flood narratives.
- **A1012:** "Flood as punishment for sin or disobedience"
- **A1015:** "Flood caused by deity or supernatural being"
- **A1018:** "Flood caused by breaking of vessel or dam"
- **A1021:** "Deluge: escape in boat (ark)"
- **A1021.2:** "Bird scouts sent out from ark"
- **A1022:** "Escape from deluge on mountain"
- **A812:** "Earth-diver" (an animal dives to the bottom of primordial waters to retrieve earth) -- closely related to many flood-recreation narratives
- **A1006.1:** "Renewal of world after world calamity"

The Motif-Index is available in full at [Internet Archive](https://archive.org/details/Thompson2016MotifIndex) and through major research libraries.

### Alan Dundes -- *The Flood Myth* (1988)

Alan Dundes, ed., *The Flood Myth* (Berkeley: University of California Press, 1988). 452 pages.

- An anthology of 26 scholarly articles on flood myths from around the world.
- Includes contributions on Genesis, Sumerian, Babylonian, Semitic, Greek, Mesoamerican, South American, Australian, and Asian flood traditions.
- Dundes himself contributed "The Flood as Male Myth of Creation," which applies psychoanalytic folkloristic interpretation, arguing that flood myths encode male anxiety about creation (women create life from water/amniotic fluid; men symbolically appropriate this creative power through the flood narrative).
- The collection represents the most comprehensive single-volume scholarly treatment of comparative flood mythology.

### Michael Witzel -- *The Origins of the World's Mythologies* (2012)

E.J. Michael Witzel, *The Origins of the World's Mythologies* (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2012). xx + 665 pages.

- **Laurasian mythology:** Witzel identifies a coherent mythological system shared by cultures from Iceland to Japan to Mesoamerica, characterized by a narrative arc from creation to destruction (cosmogony to eschatology). Flood myths are a core element of this "Laurasian" story line. Witzel traces the Laurasian system to a common source in southwestern Asia, spreading with human migration before the last glacial maximum (c. 20,000+ BP).
- **Gondwanan mythology:** A separate, older mythological stratum found primarily in sub-Saharan Africa, Australia, Melanesia, and parts of South/Southeast Asia. Gondwanan mythologies emphasize the origins of humanity in a pre-existing world but typically lack the creation-to-destruction narrative arc. Flood myths are present in Gondwanan traditions but are less structurally central.
- **Flood myths in Witzel's framework:** The flood is a key element of the Laurasian "story line" -- it appears in the sequence of world-ages, often as the mechanism of transition between ages. The Laurasian flood tradition, Witzel argues, is a single tradition that diversified as populations spread, rather than multiple independent inventions.
- **Methodology:** Witzel draws on comparative linguistics, population genetics, archaeology, and systematic comparison of mythological motifs. The work is the most ambitious synthesis of comparative mythology since Mircea Eliade.
- **Reception:** The book has been praised for its scope and ambition but criticized for potentially overestimating the reach of diffusion and underestimating independent invention. The Laurasian/Gondwanan distinction has been challenged as overly schematic.

### The Diffusionist vs. Independent Invention Debate

This is arguably the central unresolved question in the study of flood myths:

**Diffusionist position (monogenesis):**
- Flood myths derive from a single source (or a small number of sources) and spread through human migration and cultural contact.
- The structural similarities between distant flood traditions (divine warning, vessel, animal preservation, mountain landing, repopulation) are too specific to be coincidental.
- Witzel's Laurasian hypothesis is the most sophisticated modern diffusionist model.
- The strongest evidence: the detailed parallels between Mesopotamian (Atrahasis, Gilgamesh XI) and Hebrew (Genesis 6--9) flood narratives, which are almost certainly related through direct diffusion.

**Independent invention position (polygenesis):**
- Floods are universal human experiences. Every riverine and coastal civilization in history has experienced catastrophic flooding. It would be remarkable if cultures did *not* develop flood narratives.
- The "similarities" between flood traditions are often superficial: someone survives, water is involved, the world is affected. The specific details differ enormously (ark vs. hollow reed vs. hollow log vs. mountaintop vs. canoe vs. earth-diver vs. engineering).
- Some flood myths clearly have no connection to Mesopotamian/Western Asian traditions: the Chinese Gun-Yu narrative (engineering, no ark), the Australian Aboriginal traditions (sea-level rise, no divine punishment), and the Popol Vuh (design correction, no human survivor).
- The earth-diver motif in North America is structurally distinct from anything in the Old World flood tradition.

**The middle position (most modern scholars):**
- Both processes operated simultaneously. Some flood traditions are clearly related through diffusion (Mesopotamian to Hebrew to Greek, possibly). Others are clearly independent (Chinese, Australian Aboriginal, many American indigenous traditions).
- The question is not "diffusion or independent invention?" but "which traditions are related, and which are independent?" -- answered case by case through careful historical, linguistic, and archaeological analysis.
- Local geological events (river floods, tsunamis, post-glacial sea-level rise, dam-burst floods) provided the experiential raw material, which was then shaped by each culture's theological and narrative frameworks.
